The Verdict

✅
Option 1 — On-grid
Financially sound. Low risk for the owner's cash position. Proceed once net metering question is confirmed and generator–inverter integration is validated.
⚠️
Option 3 — Hybrid + Battery
Better operationally, but do not commit until battery replacement cost is quoted. At ₨2 crore plus unknown battery cycles, long-term economics need a full 25-year model.
❌
Option 2 — Hybrid (no battery)
Not recommended. The cloudy-day + grid-outage failure mode is a real biological risk for a poultry operation. The cost saving vs. Option 1 (₨17 lakh) does not justify the downside.

For the owner specifically: Under conservative assumptions (7,000 L generator diesel, ₨11.3/unit export), the owner's 50% share of savings is ~₨2.1 lakh/month against ₨2.5 lakh/month rent foregone — a net cost of roughly ₨40,000/month during the 40-month repayment period. This is manageable, and rising energy prices (10%/year modelled) make it neutral by Year 2 and positive thereafter. After 40 months you own a ₨1 crore asset and full rent resumes. If the grandfathered ₨27/unit rate applies, the deal turns positive immediately.

Most important single action: Confirm with FESCO whether the farm has or can obtain a grandfathered ₨27/unit net metering agreement. If yes, Option 1 becomes strongly attractive from Day 1. If no, the ₨11.30/unit rate still produces a viable case — the ₨40k/month shortfall is modest and temporary.

Policy risk to keep in mind: Pakistan's regulatory environment for solar has changed materially twice in 18 months (proposed cuts in 2024, abolition in Feb 2026). IEEFA warns further changes are possible. The most resilient option is to design for maximum self-consumption — meaning either Option 3 (batteries store solar for night use) or, if choosing Option 1, focusing on managing consumption to overlap with solar generation hours. Relying on export income is a regulatory risk.

If net metering is cancelled entirely (₨0/unit export): Use the "No net metering" button in the calculator to model this. Annual savings drop by ~₨9 lakh compared to the ₨11.3 rate. Option 1 remains viable on self-consumption savings alone, but the owner's monthly shortfall rises to ~₨1.2 lakh during repayment. In this scenario, Option 3 (battery) becomes relatively more attractive — batteries maximise self-consumption and eliminate export dependency altogether.

🐓 8 Questions to Verify Before Deciding

These are the specific gaps in our data. Until these are answered, the numbers above carry material uncertainty.

  1. 1
    Does the farm already hold a FESCO net metering agreement?

    If yes: what rate (₨27/unit grandfathered, or ₨11.30 new billing) and when does it expire? A grandfathered ₨27 agreement changes annual savings by up to ₨15–20 lakh/year. Contact FESCO's net metering office or your installer to check. urgent — verify before signing

  2. 2
    How many hours per day is your feeder blacked out, and at what times (day or night)?

    This drives both how much diesel is saved and how often the generator must run. Check the PITC load management portal: ccms.pitc.com.pk/FeederDetails — select Faisalabad, your grid station, and feeder "New Khanuana". currently assumed 35% diesel reduction

  3. ✓
    Diesel breakdown — confirmed.

    Of the 13,000 litres per year: 7,000 L for generators (backup during load shedding — solar can reduce this) and 6,000 L for brooder heaters (winter chick warming, night use — solar cannot replace this). The calculator uses 7,000 L as the solar-saveable baseline. owner's notes

  4. 4
    What is the total south-facing, unobstructed roof area across both sheds?

    A 100 kW system needs approximately 500–600 m² of usable roof. Confirm with the installer that the roof can structurally hold the system weight (~200–300 kg for panels + mounting). Roof strengthening could add ₨15–25 lakh. ask installer for structural assessment

  5. 5
    What is the battery bank replacement cost after 10–12 years for Option 3?

    Lithium batteries in Pakistan's hot climate (40°C+ summers) degrade faster than in cooler regions. A 25-year panel life means 2 battery replacement cycles. Without this number, Option 3's true 25-year cost cannot be calculated. critical gap for Option 3

  6. 6
    What does the generator–inverter integration software for Option 1 cost, and who provides ongoing support?

    The installer proposed third-party software to link the 60 kW generator to the on-grid inverter. Ask for: the software vendor name, annual support cost, warranty terms, and whether the inverter warranty remains valid with this modification. reliability risk

  7. 7
    Does the installer's quote include or exclude the potential 18% GST on imported solar panels?

    Pakistan is considering 18% GST on imported panels (proposal pending as of April 2026). If applied after you sign, the cost could increase by ₨8–15 lakh. Request a price-locked quote and ask specifically whether they'll absorb any future GST change. regulatory risk

  8. 8
    What is the actual peak simultaneous electrical load of both sheds at full capacity?

    The electricity bills show a maximum MDI of 64 kW (grid peak demand, October 2025). But during load shedding when the generator runs both sheds, what is the total peak kW requirement? If it exceeds 60 kW (generator capacity), there is already a supply gap — solar may help address it. max grid MDI = 64 kW from bills

⚠️ Critical Policy Change: Net Metering Was Abolished (Feb 9, 2026)

Pakistan's regulator NEPRA issued SRO 251(I)/2026 on February 9, 2026, replacing net metering with net billing.

PolicyWho it applies toExport rate (PKR/unit)Contract term
Old net meteringAgreements signed before Feb 9, 2026₨27.00Up to 10 years
New net billingNew applications from Feb 9, 2026 onward₨11.305 years

You buy grid electricity at ~₨52/kWh but can only sell surplus at ₨11.30/kWh under the new policy. This changes the economics of exporting surplus significantly.

Why did this happen? IEEFA analysis (April 2025) found that Pakistan's 5.3 GW of net-metered solar had shifted ₨159 billion in grid costs onto non-solar consumers. Power companies (DISCOs) were losing money on every unit exported at the old rate. The policy change was a predictable response — and signals that further tightening is possible. IEEFA April 2025 report

🐓 Action required before deciding: Contact FESCO immediately to confirm whether the farm already holds a net metering agreement (from a previous application or the current installer's quotes). If you have a valid grandfathered agreement at ₨27/unit, the economics are much better. If not, all calculations should use the new ₨11.30 rate. This single factor can change annual savings by 15–20 lakh PKR per year.

How electricity savings work

During daylight hours, solar covers the farm's daytime load directly — every kWh self-consumed saves ₨52 (the full grid rate including taxes). Surplus solar is exported at ₨11.30. At night, you still buy from the grid at ₨52. The self-consumption ratio — what fraction of solar production your farm actually uses in real time — is the most important assumption.

The owner's monthly net position during repayment

During the repayment period, the owner gives up ₨2.5 lakh/month in rent. But the business's energy costs drop — and the owner, as a 50% partner, receives half of those savings through increased profit. The net position:

Owner's gain (50% of annual savings ÷ 12): at ₨61.7 lakh/year savings = +₨2.57 lakh/month

Owner's cost (rent foregone): –₨2.50 lakh/month

Net: approximately break-even (+₨7,000/month) — or slightly positive from day one.

At higher savings estimates (old net metering, or higher diesel prices), the owner is meaningfully better off during repayment. At the most conservative assumptions, the deal costs the owner very little per month.

What the Research Says

IEEFA — Institute for Energy Economics & Financial Analysis (April 2025) ★★★★★

Pakistan's solar expansion has been remarkable — 5.3 GW of net-metered capacity by March 2025, helping avoid USD 12 billion in fossil fuel imports. However, IEEFA warns that the rapid growth strained the grid and shifted ₨159 billion in costs onto non-solar consumers, making the February 2026 policy reversal predictable and likely not the last. IEEFA explicitly flags that Pakistan's regulatory environment around solar remains unstable, and that future policy changes are a real investment risk for commercial installations. Source

Cambridge / World's Poultry Science Journal ★★★★

Researchers studying Pakistani poultry farms have documented the severe economic impact of the electricity crisis on poultry production: diesel costs for generators, heat stress mortality when ventilation fails, and reduced output during prolonged outages. The studies identify hybrid PV-diesel systems as the most feasible solution for broiler farms — but emphasize that any gap in power supply during summer heat events (temperatures above 40°C) causes irreversible bird losses within hours. This is the specific risk of Option 2 (hybrid without battery). Source

Academic note on payback periods ★★★

A 2022 feasibility study (Acta Mechanica Malaysia) calculated a 26–27 year payback for solar on a small poultry shed in Pakistan. This should not alarm you: the study modeled a very small system (for 32 birds), assumed pre-2022 energy prices, and did not model diesel savings. At today's energy prices — diesel at ₨520/litre and electricity at ₨52/kWh — the economics are fundamentally different. However, the absence of published case studies showing profitable 100 kW commercial poultry solar operations in Punjab is itself notable. If this investment is as obviously good as the numbers suggest, we would expect more documentation of success. This gap warrants caution alongside optimism. Source

Grid reliability context ★★★

FESCO (Faisalabad Electric Supply Company) operates a feeder classification system: high-recovery feeders receive near-zero load shedding; low-recovery feeders face 8–12 hours/day. Commercial agricultural feeders typically fall in the middle. Your specific feeder (New Khanuana 101204) can be checked at ccms.pitc.com.pk. This data is essential: if load shedding is primarily at night, solar's impact on diesel costs is lower than assumed. If it's mostly daytime, solar is far more valuable.
